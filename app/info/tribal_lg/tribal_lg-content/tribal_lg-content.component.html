2. Tribal Communities of Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu is home to 37 officially recognized Scheduled Tribes, including six Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)â€”the Todas, Kotas, Kurumbas, Irulas, Paniyas, and Kattunayakas. These communities inhabit diverse ecological zones, stretching from the Western Ghats in the southern tip to the Pulicat coast in the north, and from the Nilgiri Hills in the west to the Pichavaram mangrove forests in the east, and play a significant role in the stateâ€™s cultural and environmental heritage. The Malayali tribe is the largest, comprising nearly 45% of the tribal population, with major concentrations in districts like Salem, Tiruvannamalai, and Viluppuram.

3. Overview of Tribal Languages in Tamil Nadu

This profile identifies and collates data on 28 tribal communities in Tamil Nadu, representing at least 31 distinct mother tongues, based on triangulated sources such as Glottolog, Ethnologue, and the Peopleâ€™s Linguistic Survey of India. A overwhelming of these languages belong to the Dravidian language family, with one significant exceptionâ€”Vaagri Booli, spoken by the Narikoravan/Kuruvikkaran community, which is classified as Indo-Aryan (Bhil subgroup). The Dravidian family of languages is indigenous to South Asia; it has four primary branches: South Dravidian I, South‑Central Dravidian II, Central and North and is thought to have diversified from Proto‑Dravidian around 4,500 years ago. Within the South Dravidian branch, renowned Tamil scholar Kamil Zvelebil (1981) proposed subdivisions such as Tamiloid, Malayalamoid and Kannadoid in addition to the already then recognised Tamilâ€“Kannada, Tamilâ€“Kota, and Tamil-Malayalam sub branches. Sholaga/Soliga, spoken by the Soliga tribe, is another example; it is a Dravidian language related to Kannada and Tamil and classified under Kanndoid language.

The largest group is Malayalamoid, which includes 11 languages. These are spoken mostly in the Western Ghats region and are closely related to Malayalam, though each has its own features.

The next largest group is Tamiloid, with 7 languages. These are found in areas where tribal communities live close to Tamil-speaking regions. The languages are related to Tamil but have local differences.

Kannadoid languages make up the third-largest group, with 4 languages. These are spoken in places where there has been historical contact with Kannada-speaking areas.

This distribution shows that while most tribal languages in Tamil Nadu are Dravidian, they belong to many different branches and have developed in specific regions over time.

Out of 31 documented tribal languages in Tamil Nadu, 11 are classified as not endangered, meaning they are actively spoken across all age groups and passed down through generations. Ten languages are considered threatenedâ€”they still have active speakers but are at risk due to declining use, particularly among younger generations. Eight languages are shifting, with intergenerational transmission breaking down as children no longer learn or use them in daily life. Two languages have become extinct, with no fluent speakers remaining. Only one language is classified as vigorous, indicating stable and active use across all generations without signs of decline.

This means that two-thirds of Tamil Naduâ€™s tribal languages are either endangered, shifting, or extinct, reflecting a serious risk to their continued survival. The decline is not caused by internal community factors alone but is also shaped by systemic marginalisationâ€”such as exclusion from census categories and inadequate recognition in language policy frameworks. While some languages, like Ravula, Muthuvan, and Paniya, continue to show relative stability, others such as Malai Arayan and Kochuvelan (Ullatan) have already been declared extinct.
